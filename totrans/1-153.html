How to Apply Powerful AI Audio Models to Real-World Applications

原文：https://towardsdatascience.com/how-to-apply-powerful-ai-audio-models-to-real-world-applications/

Audio machine-learning models are powerful models that either handle audio input or can produce audio outputs. These models are important in AI because audio in the form of speech, or other sounds, is widely available, and helps us understand the world we live in. To really understand the importance of audio in the world, you can imagine the world without sound and how different it is from a world with sound.

In this article, I’ll provide a high-level overview of different audio machine learning models, the different tasks you can perform with them, and their application areas. Audio models have seen significant improvements in the last few years, especially after the LLM breakthrough with ChatGPT.

Why we need audio models

We already have extremely powerful LLMs that can deal with a lot of human interactions, so it’s important to highlight why there’s a need for audio models. I’ll highlight three main points:

  • Audio is an important dataset, just like vision and text
  • Analyzing audio directly is more expressive than analysis through transcribed text
  • Audio allows for more human-like interactions

For my first point, I think it’s important to preface that while we have both enormous datasets through text on the internet and vision through videos, we also have large amounts of data where audio is available. Most videos, for example, will contain audio that adds meaning and context to the video. Thus, if we want to create the most powerful AI models, we have to create models that can understand all modalities. Modality in this case refers to a type of data, such as

  • Text
  • Vision
  • Audio

My second point also highlights an important need for audio models. If we want to convert audio to text (so we can apply LLMs, for example), we first need to use a transcription model, which, of course, is an audio model itself. Furthermore, it will often be better to analyze audio directly, rather than analyzing a bit of audio through transcribed text. The reason for this is that the audio will capture more nuances. For example, if we have audio of someone speaking, the audio will capture the emotion of the speaker, information that can’t really be expressed through text.

Audio models also allow for more human-like experiences, for example, with the fact that you can have conversations with the AI models, instead of typing back and forth.

Audio model types

In this section, I’ll go through the main audio model types that you’ll encounter when working with audio models.

Speech-to-text

Speech-to-text is one of the most common use cases for audio models, and is also called transcription. Speech-to-text is the task where you input speech and output the text provided in the speech. This is incredibly important to summarize meeting notes, or when you’re talking to a virtual assistant like Siri on your phone. Speech-to-text is also used to create larger training datasets for LLMs.

You can use speech-to-text models to take in audio clips for analysis. For example, suppose you have a customer service interaction. In that case, you can transcribe this interaction and perform text analysis on it, such as analyzing the length of the interaction, quickly analyzing the performance of the customer service representative, or seeing if the customer was happy with the interaction, without having to hear through the entire interaction. Analyzing text is usually way faster than analyzing the audio, since you can read text faster than you can listen to the audio of it. You can see an example of such a transcribed interaction below:

[Customer service representative]
Hi, thanks for calling, what do you need help with?

[Customer]
Hi, I need a refund for a recent purchase I made

[Customer service representative]
Okay, do you have the order ID for the purchase?

...

However, it is important to note that when you’re converting speech to text, you are losing some information, as I described in the intro to this article. You’ll lose the emotion of the people speaking in the audio, and it will thus be hard to determine the customer’s emotions from the customer service interaction, unless the emotion is clearly communicated through text. In either case, you’ll lose nuance from the audio, simply because reading through the text of a conversation can never be as expressive as listening to the conversation itself.

Thus, if you want to perform a deeper analysis of the audio, you can perform direct audio analysis of the interaction, instead of first transcribing the interaction to text. For example, if you want to determine the emotion of the customer in the interaction, you can feed in the audio directly, together with a prompt such as below. You can then perform direct audio analysis, capturing further nuance.

prompt = 
"""Analyse the emotional state of the customer in this interaction

{audio_clip}

"""

Text-to-speech

Text-to-speech is another important use case for audio models. This is the reverse of the previously described task, where you instead input text and generate audio for this text. In the same way you lose information transcribing text, you now need to add information to create the audio.

Therefore, you’ll often have to provide the emotion the generated speech should be in when performing text-to-speech (unless the provider automatically determines emotion when generating the audio).

Text-to-speech can be useful in many scenarios:

  • Creating advertisements, where you want to do a voice-over, given a transcript. This can easily be done using services like Elevenlabs
  • For customer service interactions, by having a voice, customers can talk to. You can, for example, have the customer call in, transcribe their text (speech-to-text), use an LLM to generate a response (text-to-text), and generate audio from the LLM response (text-to-speech)

The approach in the last bullet point works from a quality perspective. However, if you do this, you’ll probably encounter latency issues, since it takes time to both transcribe text and respond with an LLM before you stream in the audio response. You’ll thus probably want to utilize speech-to-speech models instead, which I’ll talk about in the next section.

Speech-to-speech

Speech-to-speech models are powerful models capable of both inputting and outputting speech. This is super useful in live scenarios, where you need to create rapid responses.

You can, for example, create direct customer service representatives with speech-to-speech models, directly responding to user queries with low delay. In such interactions, the delay is super important, considering you want to create a human-like interaction for the customer. The interaction should, in theory, feel the same, if not better, than dealing with a human customer service representative.

Optimally, you’ll use a direct speech-to-speech model, such as Qwen-3-Omni. An alternative would be to first perform speech-to-text, text-to-text (with an LLM), and then text-to-speech. However, it’s important to preface that it’s almost always better to use an end-to-end model (such as speech-to-speech in this case), instead chaining different models together. This is because end-to-end models will retain information better, thus providing better outputs.

Another speech-to-speech model I’d like to mention is voice cloning. This is the application where you provide an audio sample of one particular voice. You can then generate new audio with the cloned voice by providing text for a voice-over. Voice-to-voice models have also seen vast improvements in the last few years, and can be useful to quickly generate a lot of voice-overs.

For example, imagine you want to create an audiobook from a textbook, with a specific voice that has done previous audiobooks. Normally, you would have to book a recording room and have the voice narrate the whole new book, which would take weeks. Instead, if you have a lot of samples from this voice already, you can now generate a full voice-over in a matter of minutes using voice cloning models. Naturally, you always need to obtain permissions before using a voice-cloning model.

Conclusion

In this article, I’ve discussed different voice models, with speech-to-text and text-to-speech. and speech-to-speech models, which are all useful in their own application areas. I think voice models will see continued development and improvements, given their importance. Audio models are important because audio is an important modality to understanding the world, just like text and vision are. I believe audio is similar to images, where it is hard to describe only using words.
