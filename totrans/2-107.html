Why BI in the AI Age

原文：https://towardsdatascience.com/why-bi-in-the-ai-age/

AI forces us analysts to stare in the mirror and ask what exactly is the value produced by making and sharing charts by hand.

LLMs can generate queries and code to create compelling charts from natural language prompts in seconds. They know syntax we have forgotten, packages we’ve never heard of, and are all too eager to respond to every executive demand for charts at all hours of the day!

But what’s in a chart?

Fundamentally, a chart compresses a bunch of data into an easy-to-understand insight that people can hopefully take action on. The viewer of the chart needs to trust that the creator1 of the chart made sure it was correct2 and appropriate3. Otherwise, the viewer would need to go through their own analytics process to get to the insight themselves.

So let’s explore the parts of the chart-making process where AI still falls short — not just to point out its current limitations, but to highlight where human analysts add the most value. If you’re working in BI, this article will help you better understand how to partner with AI tools, where to stay hands-on, and how to design your workflows and data models so that both humans and machines can generate more reliable insights.

At DataCamp, we think a lot about what great analytics looks like in the age of AI. As a platform that teaches both the technical and strategic sides of modern BI, we’ve seen firsthand how the best outcomes come from combining AI speed with human judgment. This article shares some of the key patterns we’ve observed and the practical takeaways we believe BI professionals should carry forward.

4 areas where AI falls short in the chart making process

  1. Lack of metadata
  2. Lack of extra data
  3. Understanding emerges through exploration
  4. Compression is lossy and arbitrary

Lack of Metadata

Consider this simple sales dataset:

An AI prompt like “show me sales by region” would instantly generate a chart:

And this chart seems totally reasonable. Why question it?

Well, let’s look at the counterfactual of having a human answer this same question. While they may just immediately produce the same or similar chart, they more likely will find important metadata context during their process of exploring the data en route to creating the chart:

  • customer_id actually represents both individual customers AND corporate accounts (requiring potentially different analysis approaches)
  • amount represents total contract value booked, not cash received or revenue recognized (crucial for financial reporting)
  • sales_rep NULL values indicate channel partner sales (a completely different business model)

You asked for ‘sales by region’ and see in the chart that the AI produced that SE is underperforming. The obvious next step would be to focus on improving the SE sales team. But the human analyst’s metadata investigation reveals this would be the wrong move entirely.

Digging deeper, they could discover that SE’s poor performance is actually driven by collections issues on partner sales (those NULL sales_rep values), not direct sales execution. The region isn’t failing—the partner channel is struggling. 

So the analyst version of the chart may look more like this:

This isn’t exactly what you prompted for, and that’s the point.

Without this human investigation that went a bit beyond your ask, you’d be hammering the wrong sales team about performance when you should be reaching out to your channel partners. In addition, these findings also have implications for the data model. You found that you need several transformations and metadata embedded in this table so that it can more reliably be used by an LLM or another human to use this data to create a chart.

It should be mentioned that many BI tools are adopting various techniques for giving LLMs more metadata access to things like schemas, data dictionaries, and dbt, but these all assume the metadata has been written down, which is often not the case.

Lack of Extra Data

There’s data AI knows, data AI doesn’t know, data AI knows it doesn’t know, and data AI doesn’t know it doesn’t know4. 

The data an AI needs in order to interpret a chart or metric may be completely unexpected and hard to proactively make available. Let’s take an expanded version of the same dataset and have an AI try to explain a revenue dip in March 2024:

These suggestions are all worth potentially looking into and the overall recommendation of it being anomalous makes sense. However, an experienced analyst will likely have instincts to the true root cause. For instance, the human analyst may know of some data that exist outside of the database that they feel confident could be the reason:

  • External Market Event: Your main competitor had a major promotion that month
  • Internal Activity: Your pricing team ran an A/B test that accidentally excluded 30% of prospects
  • Historical Information: A key integration partner changed their API, breaking sign-up flows for two weeks

These discoveries require data that is spread across an organization and its ecosystem, not just within the database that’s connected to a BI tool. 

This allows us to better understand what’s going on in the data vs a less informed guess. In the future, more streams of information will certainly be factored into LLM context windows and many teams are evaluating how to design their data to be more digestible by AI. However, in the present, to try to determine causal relationships you need the human analyst’s instinct for generating probable hypotheses and confirming theories.

Understanding emerges through exploration

Real datasets are messy and oftentimes the cleaning of the data is key to coming to the right answer. Let’s revisit our sales data with a couple more fields related to orders:

Now you have to make some cleaning decisions: 

  • Do you count ACME as one $2500 sale or track their purchasing pattern over time?
  • What does that NULL amount in the second ACME record mean — a return, a pending renewal, or data entry error?

Well, you could ask the AI what to do, and we did:

An analyst is often put in this position to reconcile and then codify how multiple teams want the business logic to be written. After talking with accounting and sales they determine: “ACME has a renewal with no amount recorded — they’re probably negotiating terms or there’s a processing delay.” The analyst can then decide whether to exclude this record, estimate the amount, or create a separate renewal analysis track.

This could be a one-off decision or something that gets factored into the data model. If we had just asked the AI for a chart on average sale amount we might never have bumped into and cleaned this oddity. Again, a human poking around to get an answer finds opportunities to make the data better for everyone including AI.

Compression is Lossy and Arbitrary

Even when AI returns a completely correct chart it may not be the right chart. The same sales data tells completely different stories depending on presentation. Here’s how our sales performance data could be presented:

Just the Number — “Total Sales: $1.2M” (Sounds good! But compared to what? What was it last month?)

Basic Bar Chart — Sales by region showing SE lagging behind NE and SW (confirms your bias that the SE team needs help)

Fully Annotated and Contextualized — The same chart, but with partner sales segmented out, showing SE direct sales are actually strong and the issue is the partner channel payment processing bug that started in Q3. Industry benchmark and growth trend lines show overall performance is excellent.

(Note: all these charts were generated by Claude, an AI assistant created by Anthropic.)

To be clear, you can use AI to create any of these (like I did!). But when people self-serve with AI, will they get to an appropriate level of depth and understanding or stop at just getting the number? The AI might give users the misleading middle version, but if routed to a human analyst, they would likely know to dig deeper and present the data in a way that leads to the right action. 

As complete as this final chart feels, this is still just one of many ways to understand this data. We could have segmented by customer size, looked at monthly trends, filtered out renewals, etc. We could have also looked at this data by week or day. The focus on regional totals without segmenting partner vs. direct sales is somewhat arbitrary, and the monthly time grain is compressing daily volatility that might reveal when the partner payment issue started.

An analyst often makes many charts before settling on the one presented and can thus caveat, contextualize, or quickly respond to why this chart was picked and help people follow up with more questions. Presenting one chart that does not show the whole picture is a feature, not a bug, of data visualization. It allows you to present a focused, understandable view into the data. 

Ultimately, as an analyst you must ask yourself: are you comfortable with this chart representing all the underlying data? Are you ready to present this chart for others to reliably draw conclusions from?

Keep Calm, and Query On

Great analytics isn’t about generating charts quickly, it’s about building confidence in decisions through rigorous investigation of data. Every discovery, design choice, and contextual annotation represents a human analyst’s business intelligence (is this even a pun? If so: intended).

In the AI age, the analysts who thrive will be those who embrace AI as a powerful tool to help write code while recognizing that the most valuable analytical work—the thinking, questioning, and contextualizing—remains fundamentally human.
